## THE ARC (the one thing to internalize)
**Gulfport was born of this waterfront — and Channel South returns it to the water, on the exact ground where the city's hospitality began.** Railroad → port → the Great Southern Hotel (the original grand "front door" to the coast) → demolition for the highway → Katrina's ground zero → today's downtown renaissance. Channel South isn't a new building dropped on a lot. It's the **waterfront coming full circle.** That's the narrative spine of everything.

---

## 1 · HOW GULFPORT WAS BORN (1887–1906)
- **1887:** Capt. **William H. Hardy** conceives Gulfport while surveying his **Gulf & Ship Island Railroad** — looking at millions of acres of unharvested **long-leaf yellow pine** in south Mississippi, he draws a line from Ship Island harbor to meet his rail. A city, willed into being to move timber to the sea.
- **1897:** **Capt. Joseph T. Jones** — a Pennsylvania/Philadelphia oil baron and Civil War veteran — arrives on the coast (Feb 14, 1897), captivated by Hardy's project. He **personally finances the dredging of the channel and harbor.**
- **1898:** Gulfport incorporated. **1902:** the harbor opens.
- **1906:** Gulfport ships **293 million feet of lumber — the largest lumber-export city in the country.** The waterfront *was* the economy.

**Why it matters:** the water is not a backdrop to Gulfport — it's the reason Gulfport exists. Channel South's whole premise (returning the city to its waterfront) is historically true, not marketing.

---

## 2 · THE GREAT SOUTHERN HOTEL (1902–1951) — the original of what Channel South is becoming
- Built **1902–03 by Joseph T. Jones**, designed by **Thomas Sully** (the celebrated New Orleans architect, a Gulf Coast native).
- Conceived as a **"pleasure palace"** for wealthy Chicagoans and New Yorkers escaping winter — Gulfport as a *destination,* not a stopover.
- **250 rooms** (a bath for every two), **telephones in every room**, hot & cold running water, billiards, **gardens, a tennis court** — astonishing luxury for 1903.
- Jones built an **impressive pier with a tram** running out to the **Gulfport Yacht Club** at its end (clubhouse burned 1905, promptly rebuilt).
- **1908:** the family adds the **Donald Ross-designed golf course** — the **oldest in Mississippi** (closed 2022 after 114 years).
- Hit hard by the **Depression**, closed briefly, then **demolished in 1951** "in the name of progress" as **U.S. Route 90** was built along the beach.

**The through-line to Channel South:** the Great Southern was the coast's first grand waterfront destination — a hotel that drew the world to Gulfport. **Hotel Holden is its spiritual successor on the very same ground** — and its rooftop overlooks **Jones Park, named for the man who built the original.** That's not a coincidence you invented; it's true. Lean on it.

---

## 3 · THE FADE & THE STORM (1951, 2005)
- **1951:** demolition of the Great Southern for the highway closes the waterfront's golden age. The grand destination era ends.
- **August 2005 — Hurricane Katrina:** ~120 mph winds, a storm surge near **30 feet**, **238 killed in Mississippi**, the coast left as concrete slabs. Gulfport's port and waterfront were **"ground zero."**
- **Recovery:** 900,000+ volunteers, a Port of Gulfport Restoration Program, tax incentives to "build back better," and a long, ongoing rebuild (20 years on in 2025).

> **SENSITIVITY — handle Katrina with care.** This audience *lived* it. Use it as **resilience and return**, never as tragedy-marketing or a hook. The right note is quiet pride: *the waterfront comes back.* When in doubt, imply rather than state, and let the renaissance — not the storm — carry the message.

---

## 4 · THE RENAISSANCE CHANNEL SOUTH BELONGS TO (now)
Channel South is **not alone** — it's the flagship of a real downtown Gulfport revival, which is part of *why* the press is engaged:
- **The Markham Hotel** (built 1926, vacant since Katrina) **reopened April 2026** after a landmark restoration — 115 rooms, the Library restaurant, a rooftop **Upstairs Bar** with beach/sunset views. *(Note the parallel: a historic hotel reborn with a rooftop — Channel South/Hotel Holden is the next, larger chapter of the same story.)*
- **The White House Hotel** — earlier restoration by the same investor group.
- **Jones Park** — "**Mississippi's Front Yard**," 60 acres, a 319-slip marina, waterfront promenade, and the 23,000 sq ft Barksdale Pavilion, renewed after Katrina. **This is the view from Hotel Holden's rooftop.**

**Why it matters:** position Channel South as **the anchor of downtown Gulfport's return to the water** — in good company, leading the wave, on the most storied ground of all.
